General Advice

Revision Techniques are widely varied and dependent on the person, subject, specific module, exam board etc. However, there are some universal techniques that should be considered for any subject.

- Spaced Repetition is the art of learning content by repeatedly testing your knowledge. Whether this is through making flashcards or doing mind-dumps (there are many other names for this; it is where you write out as much as you know about a topic, check how much you got correct, then write it all out again to hopefully get more correct) or any other way of actively revisiting content. Ideally you should learn everything before you start applying it to exam-style practice, so by constantly "relearning" everything, over time it will become infinitely easier to memorise.

- Teaching Others seems to be a heavily underlooked technique. Your teachers' knowledge of the subject you are studying is far beyond yours, thanks to their years of experience and practice. You can follow in this path by taching someone what you are attempting to revise, i.e. a fellow classmate, friend, family member or even just recording yourself asking and answering questions. This way you can act as if you're participating in the classroom no matter where you are, which is of course the backbone of your education.

- Exam Practice should go without saying. Your grade is completely determined by the last ever set of exams you do, so naturally you should be confidently completing past papers, practice papers, specimen papers etc. by the time you reach that point. For Maths and Further Maths specifically, you should be spending a very large majority of your revision time practicing and practicing. Most, if not all, Exam Boards will have some pattern in the way they ask questions. The earlier you begin doing exam questions the more time you will have to complete and repeat them; eventually you will recognise these patterns and find many difficult questions far easier.

Some Resources you should utilise are: YouTube Channels i.e. Khan Academy and Organic Chemistry Tutor, Flashcard creators i.e. Quizlet, AnkiWeb and Gizmo, School/College Resources if available can be so incredibly useful, specifically because they tend to be far more personalised and targeted towards what you are currently learning.

Maths and Further Maths

Maths is the most commonly chosen A-Level subject, and for good reason. It is a subject that compliments nearly every other, it will also (probably) be the easiest subject you take as long as you know how to properly study it. If you study any of Physics, Chemistry or Biology alongside Maths you will find some major topics overlap especially in Applied Maths i.e. Forces, Moments, Hypothesis tests and Linear Regression. Of course I could include Further Maths in this list but I assume the overlap there is self-explanatory. You will apply mathematics in so many concepts during your studies it'll almost become like another language to you.

With other subjects you will probably spend much more time actually learning and understanding a concept before applyig that knowledge into exam practice. With pure maths, as long as you generally understand the fundamentals of Integration, Differentiation and Trigonometric Identities/Functions; you can just rinse and repeat hundreds of practice questions and you'll guarantee yourself 50% of your grade. Mechanics and Statistics is slightly more demanding of memorisation, especially with difficult Moments and Hypothesis Testing questions, but essentially the same applies. Further Maths is of course far more challenging as the topics tend to be tougher to grasp. But a heavy lean on Matrices, Vectors, Complex Numbers and Differential Equations will be of immense support as these cover the bulk of exam questions in the Pure section; within Mechanics and Statistics it'll be most worth your time studying Elastic Collisions in 1D and 2D, Impulse and Momentum, Circular Motion, Centres of Mass, Poisson and Geometric Distributions, Chi-squared tests and T/Z-Distributions.

50% probably won't suffice, though, so to fight for A and A* grades you'll need to utilise the environment you are in. There will likely be many students in your cohort, if not there's plenty on social media, that are experts at studying and are willing to help- this is of course in addition to the support you are entitled to by your teachers. You can fully reach your potential if you can confidently seek out help. The other side of available support is probably even more beneficial: the internet.

There are many websites that can offer you high quality revision material for free, but I would recommend Physics & Maths Tutor as a go-to spot. There you can access all the exam practice you need: past papers, practice papers, written worksheets, cheatsheets and model solutions.

On a similar note, the majority of mathematical inquiries you have will be answered on YouTube. For both Maths and Further Maths you shall discover every niche bit of content will be available to you for free on YouTube, explained in beautiful detail by experts. Biology

Biology as an A-Level subject is often found by students to be one of (if not the most) intriguing and dynamic, exploring the phenomena of life around them, as well as that within them. It is usually picked up by those who would like to explore the processes of life and their interaction at the cell, organism, organ and environmental levels. There is a considerable overlap of Biology with other branches of science, mostly so with Chemistry, especially when it comes to the concepts of biochemistry and molecular biology which require a solid understanding of chemical reactions. In the same vein, if you pursue Psychology or Physical Education along with biology, you will understand how they interrelate at the level of human body systems, nervous systems, and genes.

To put it simply, biology is tough. Unlike in Maths, which is all about doing an abundance of practice questions until everything clicks, biology demands the mastery of an enormous range of concepts which then have to be used under different conditions. Be prepared to have to recollect all of: cells and their structures and functions, principles of heredity, ecological systems, evolution, and even the biochemistry of photosynthesis and respiration week after week. Consistently achieving good test results requires a proper understanding and an advanced approach to the subject. You must learn how to study without breaks, that is, very active revision, and break information down into its finest details so you can analyse the process of how you should build your answers.

Another priority should be proper exam technique. Understanding the exam format, the question types, the format of the answers, and the structure of the exam from the multiple-choice bouts through data interpretation sections to exhaustingly long essay parts is very important. Utilize old papers for this purpose and try to practice more how you would write clearly and simply about complex topics that have many biological processes in them. All the help you can get from your teachers, online materials, and study partners will be very beneficial; it is very easy to get burnt out in biology if you try to cram everything by yourself.

There are many websites that I would recommend students use as a staple in their studies, a certain bunch I would like to mention in particular are A-Level Biology, Khan Academy High School Biology and Seneca Learning, which can help teach you via quizzes, flashcards and revision notes. As for what you can find on YouTube, simply searching your inquiries will spawn hundreds of relevant videos carefully crafted by highly passionate individuals. Chemistry

Chemistry is considered as the “central science”, as it is important in the linkage of the physical and biological sciences. With regards to A-level study, Chemistry is one of those subjects that will require you to engage both your logical and creative thinking, therefore find its best companions in Biology, Physics or even Maths. There is a considerable overlap when you study Biology and Chemistry, for example in the organization of macromolecules and schemes of metabolism. There are also atomic and molecular theories taught in Physics that will be useful in understanding some of the aspects of Chemistry.

Chemistry A-Level is split up into three divisions- physical, organic and inorganic chemistry. It is a discipline that is based on the understanding of the basic skills in atomic structures, chemical bonds, thermodynamic reviews, reaction rates, kinetic studies, and chemical interactions. Organic Chemistry is in many cases regarded as the most difficult quarter because it involves learning numerous reactions and their mechanisms. However, making sense of the causes and the structure of these chemical reactions can help ease the burden.

In order to achieve the best grades, it is a necessity to put yourself into practice. You should get used to the practice of working with theory and applying it in practical problems and calculations. Cramming frequently asked questions, practicing past exam papers, utilising relevant textbooks, and solving quantitative problems will comfortably put your exam skills at a competitive edge. Sites like chemguide and A-Level Chemistry provide heaps of detailed revision notes and practice questions to aid you in your studies.

YouTube is an invaluable tool for visual learners, with numerous channels offering step-by-step explanations and demonstrations of key concepts. Below are some great channels to check out:

Physics

Physics is by many considered to be on a similar difficulty level to Further Maths, but rightfully so, as it is an incredibly rewarding A-Level subject- especially for those interested in a career in engineering. Physics will provide you with a deeper understanding of how the universe and mostly everything within it functions. It is a subject that pairs perfectly with Maths and Further Maths, especially in mechanics where most of the content is the same. You'll also find significant overlap if you're studying Chemistry, specifically in atomic structures and thermodynamics.

In physics you need to have a solid grasp of the fundamentals. Regardless of what exam board you take, you'll be encountering lots and lots of Mechanics, Electricity, Waves, and Quantum Physics. Understanding these topics will be no easy task, but a mix of constant content repetition and constant exam practice (the worst of both worlds unfortunately) will place you in a good spot.

Use a variety of resources like textbooks, past papers, and online tutorials to strengthen your understanding. The support from your teachers and peers is invaluable, and online platforms like Physics & Maths Tutor and Isaac Physics offer extensive practice materials, past papers, and exam-style questions for free.

Computer Science

Computer Science is a rapidly growing A-Level subject, with more and more people discovering how easily it synergises with neighbouring STEM subjects. It is an ideal choice for students that have a love and intrigue for technology and are willing to use it as a compliment to mathematical knowledge to solve tricky but exciting problems. Physics is also a strong compliment to Computer Science and is a common A-Level pairing, you will find many overlaps in computational modelling and numerical methods.

A-Level Computer Science is split up into theoretical and practical aspects of computing. You'll cover data structures, algorithms, computer systems, networks, databases, and the theory of computation. Mastering these concepts requires a blend of logical thinking, problem-solving skills, and hands-on experience with programming languages like Python, Java, and/or C++. You will learn equally as much (if not more) outside of the classroom as it is a very research intensive subject that requires students be confident in using the internet to its limit.

In order to succeed in Computer Science, you should practice coding regularly to help develop your understanding of both theory and application. It is very easy to forget concepts if you don't regularly revisit them, so challenging yourself on platforms like LeetCode or Codeforces, will not only force you to retain valuable knowledge for your studies but also hopefully further your interest in the subject, which you can use to apply externally in the form of personal projects. Doing this along with regularly reviewing past papers will help you drastically (this technique carries on into University level CS too).

Some of my website recommendations would be GeeksforGeeks, W3Schools, and Stack Overflow; they each offer an abundance of coding practice as well as solutions to mostly any obstacle you may come across. Some great YouTube channels to visit:

- Computerphile

- CS50 by Harvard University
